Privacy Decisions with LLM

Designing contextual nudges to guide privacy-conscious decision-making in social media sharing

Challenge

Social media users frequently share content without considering privacy implications. How can we use AI-generated contextual nudges to promote more thoughtful privacy decisions without increasing cognitive burden?

Solution

I developed an innovative approach using one-shot prompt engineering to generate contextual privacy nudges, then conducted a comprehensive study to evaluate their effectiveness on user behavior and cognitive load.

Role: Lead Researcher & Designer
Timeline: 4 months
Methods: Online Survey, A/B Testing, Thematic Analysis
Tools: Qualtrics, ChatGPT, MAXQDA, Prolific

Research Methodology

Data Collection

  • Prolific participant recruitment (US users)
  • 68 memes across 13 privacy categories
  • Likert scale sharing likelihood ratings
  • Cognitive load and VOPP measurements

Experimental Design

  • Between-subjects design (4 conditions)
  • Generalized Latin square distribution
  • No nudge, universal, categorical, granular
  • OODA framework-based nudge structure

Analysis

  • Kruskal-Wallis test for non-parametric data
  • Dunn's post-hoc with Bonferroni correction
  • Inductive thematic analysis in MAXQDA
  • Two-coder consensus approach

Demonstrated how AI-generated nudges can provide personalized privacy guidance at scale, offering a practical solution for technology platforms.

Ethical AI Design Framework

Cognitive Load Optimization

Proved that well-designed privacy interventions can simultaneously improve decision quality while reducing mental burden on users.

Key Insights

  • Granular contextual nudges most effective - providing specific, context-aware guidance reduced privacy-violating sharing decisions by 13%.
  • Cognitive load reduction achieved - well-designed nudges decreased mental effort by 16% while improving decision quality.

UX Research Impact

Decision Framework Development

Established foundational framework for communication transparency in AI interactions, providing design principles for adaptive verification systems.

User Mental Model Insights

Uncovered how users naturally assess stakes and domain familiarity when interacting with AI, informing more intuitive AI assistant design.

Product Feature Recommendations

Provided actionable insights for improving session retention and subscription conversion through matched user expectations.

Decision-Making Framework Discovered

  • Stake Assessment: "What's at stake?" and "Is it reversible?" - Users evaluate potential consequences before deciding to verify.
  • Domain Familiarity: "How much do I know about this?" - Users leverage their existing expertise to gauge AI reliability.
  • Verification Levels: Four distinct patterns emerged - Minimal, Quick Check, Moderate, and Extensive verification based on stakes and familiarity.
  • Communication Transparency: Vocal interventions that match user expectations reduce friction while promoting necessary verification.

UX Research Impact

Confidence Calibration Guidelines

Discovered the "Goldilocks Effect" - users prefer moderate confidence levels, providing design principles for AI communication that balances authority with trustworthiness.

Human-AI Trust Framework

Established that users apply interpersonal trust criteria to AI interactions, informing more human-centered approaches to conversational AI design.

Evidence-Based Communication

Demonstrated that users value specific evidence over confidence claims, shifting focus from assertive language to credible data presentation in AI responses.

Key Insights

  • The "Goldilocks Effect": Users prefer moderate confidence - not too pushy, not too uncertain. Extreme confidence triggers skepticism while excessive hedging reduces credibility.
  • "Show, Don't Tell": Users value specific evidence over confidence claims. Data references and credible details matter more than assertive language.
  • Human Standards Applied: Participants judge voice assistants using interpersonal trust criteria - looking for honesty, expertise, and appropriate social cues.
